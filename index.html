whoa, the pre-work phase is already over! it's been a lot of work learning how to use these new tools, and there's still a lot i haven't fully grasped.

i like that the long-term, widespread usage of HTML, CSS, and Javascript has driven development of a powerful variety of capabilities, and that there are active communities developing and improving these tools. on the flip side, this means that there's a huge number of elements, attributes, and functions to learn about, which feels daunting. fortunately, i'm walking a well-worn path, so it seems easy to find documentation and help when necessary.

it's cool that these three separate languages developed in tandem for handling these three aspects of web development. i do think it works well, and i've been structuring my workflow around these three aspects:

  1. build the basic HTML structure of the page
  2. do a bit of prettifying with CSS
  3. plug in all the functionality with Javascript

i think i'll try flipping steps 2 and 3 next time.

i wonder what it would be like if one language had developed to handle all these aspects. would it be easier to use, without juggling different syntax? would it even work? does such a language exist?

my biggest challenge during pre-work has been wrangling my creative ideas. the ideas i have on top of the MVP have had me spending more time than strictly necessary on a few of the projects. still, that's not such a bad thing, because i enjoy thinking creatively and figuring out how to do stuff.

our daily standups were the biggest help during the pre-work phase. it's been great to be able to ask questions every day. reporting on my progress to the group regularly has helped me keep my projects moving.

this part-time phase has also been helpful for figuring out what i have the capacity to do, how to prioritize my goals, and how to maintain balance. i hope i can continue to do this going into the full-time phase, although it's clear to me that it's going to be hard!

javascript is making sense to me too. based on the kata practice, i know that i don't always come up with the most elegant, simple solutions for a given problem, but i'm sure i'll learn better methods over time. i'm also moving slow with the syntax - it's pretty different from the languages i have the most experience with - but that will come with time and practice too.

i'm enjoying discovering the interplay between these three languages. i like learning about how they've developed over time, how the standards evolve, and how the different browsers handle them. it's neat to learn about best practices for managing their interplay on a website, and the factors that inform developers' decisions.

this week we practiced some intense pseudocoding. i liked it! this exercise was hypothetical - we pseudocoded something we aren't actually going to build - so i took the opportunity to do something fun. i dreamed up a scifi bot (the ensweaterator) that searches for people who are cold, then knits sweaters directly onto their bodies.

because this exercise was hypothetical, i had a little fun and made some decisions about my bot that wouldn't be practical or ethical in real life. some of these decisions, like making the bot a drone so that it could knit sweaters in mid-air, were just for fun. others decisions - including that there is no way for a person to give consent to be ensweaterated, and very little regard for the person's safety - are commentary on some people's approaches to developing new technology (approaches that i wholeheartedly oppose in real life).

still, there was a lot to do, and at times it was a struggle. this was especially true on wednesday, when it came time to brainstorm and begin the pseudocoding exercise. it took me a while to decide what to pseudocode, and then to wrangle my creative brain into a state of focus and execution. i don't think this was just because my robot was hypothetical; i'm sure the same would be true of a new software idea with open-ended possibilities. i have the impression that some of my classmates struggled with the same thing.
